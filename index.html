An Efficient Beam Search Algorithm for Active Perception in Mobile Robotics

Kaixian Qu, Han Wang, Victor Klemm, Cesar Cadena, Marco Hutter
Robotic Systems Lab, ETH Zurich
International Journal of Robotics Research (IJRR)
Paper arXiv Video Code

NBS autonomously navigates a robot through cluttered environments, reconstructing surfaces by balancing exploration of unknown regions with exploitation of known informative viewpoints.

Abstract

Active perception is a fundamental problem in autonomous robotics in which the robot must decide where to move and what to sense in order to obtain the most informative observations for accomplishing its mission. Existing approaches either solve a computationally expensive traveling salesman problem over heuristically selected informative nodes, or adopt a more efficient but overly constrained shortest path tree formulation.

To address these limitations, we explore beam search algorithms as scalable alternatives. While the standard beam search provides scalability by preserving the top-B paths at each depth level, it is prone to local optima and exhibits parameter sensitivity. Our first contribution is a node-wise beam search (NBS) algorithm, which maintains top-B candidates per node to enable more effective exploration of the solution space. Systematic benchmarking on graphs shows that NBS consistently outperforms other baselines and maintains strong performance even at low beam widths.

As a second contribution, we integrate the concept of frontiers into the path selection criterion, introducing the expected gain metric, which better balances exploration and exploitation compared to existing alternatives.

Our third contribution proposes the rapidly-exploring random annulus graph (RRAG), a novel graph construction method that preserves full orientation sampling and ensures connectivity in cluttered environments through a fallback local sampling-based planner.

Extensive experiments demonstrate that NBS combined with RRAG achieves the highest performance across all three representative active perception tasks, outperforming state-of-the-art algorithms by at least 20% in one or more tasks. We further validate the approach on real robotic platforms in different scenarios.

Problem Formulation

In active perception, a robot must decide where to move and what to observe in order to maximize the information gained about its environment. We formulate this as a budget-constrained path optimization on a directed graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, g, c)$ with vertex gains $g(v) \geq 0$ and positive edge costs $c(e) > 0$. The objective is to find a path $p^*$ starting from $v_s$ that maximizes the accumulated gain under a cost budget:

$$p^* = \arg\max_{p \in \mathcal{P}} \; g(p) \quad \text{subject to} \quad c(p) \leq C$$

where $g(p)$ aggregates gains over the set of unique vertices visited and $c(p)$ sums edge costs along the path. We study this problem under three progressively more realistic settings:

  1. Graph a priori. The graph is fully known in advance. The robot plans a path that maximizes total gain within the budget.
  2. Graph online perception. The graph exists but is discovered incrementally through local sensing within a perception radius $\rho$. The policy must adapt as new vertices and edges are revealed, balancing exploitation of known high-gain regions with exploration of the unknown.
  3. Active perception. No graph is given. The robot operates in continuous 3D space and must autonomously construct a graph within the discovered collision-free space $\mathcal{X}_{\mathrm{free}}$ while simultaneously planning and navigating to maximize a task-dependent objective such as surface reconstruction or volumetric exploration.

(i) Full graph known; optimal path drawn

(ii) Nodes discovered as robot moves

(iii) Graph built from free space

We seek an algorithm that performs well across all three settings. The contributions below address the path selection strategy (NBS, Expected Gain) and the graph construction method (RRAG) needed for the active perception setting.

Method Overview

Node-wise Beam Search (NBS)

Motivation. Depth-wise beam search (DBS) maintains the top $B$ paths at each search depth, but this makes it prone to local optima—once the entire beam converges on regions with high immediate gains, it neglects other promising candidates. In the worst case, all $B$ paths in the beam may terminate at the same node.

Key idea. NBS instead maintains the top $B$ paths per node. This ensures that at least one promising path remains available at every visited node, encouraging exploration across the graph rather than focusing on immediate high-gain regions.

Algorithm: Node-wise Beam Search (NBS)

Input: Graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$, start node $v_s$
Parameters: beam width $B$, search depth $D$, path quality function $q$

  1. Initialize best path $p^* \leftarrow (v_s)$, per-node beam $\textbf{open}[v_s] \leftarrow \{(v_s)\}$
  2. for depth $d = 1$ to $D$ do
    1. Initialize empty per-node heap $\textbf{heap}$
    2. for each node $v \in \mathcal{V}$ with $\textbf{open}[v] \neq \emptyset$ do
      1. for each path $p \in \textbf{open}[v]$ do
        1. for each outgoing edge $(v, v') \in \delta^+(v)$ not already traversed in $p$ do
          • Extend: $p' \leftarrow p + (v, v')$
          • if $c(p') > C$: skip (budget exceeded)
          • if $q(p') > q(p^*)$: update $p^* \leftarrow p'$
          • Insert $p'$ into $\textbf{heap}[v']$, keeping only top-$B$ paths per node
    3. $\textbf{open} \leftarrow \textbf{heap}$
  3. return $p^*$

Complexity. NBS has time complexity $O(|\mathcal{E}|\,D\,B\,(D + \log B))$ and space complexity $O(|\mathcal{V}|\,D\,B)$. Even at beam width $B=1$, NBS delivers strong and robust performance due to its per-node diversity guarantee.

Expected Gain: Balancing Exploration and Exploitation

The choice of selection criterion determines how candidate paths are ranked. We consider three criteria:

1. Path Gain. The most direct criterion, maximizing accumulated gain:

$$q(p) = g(p) = \sum_{v \in \{v_i \mid v_i \in p\}} g(v)$$

2. Path Ratio. A commonly used heuristic that optimizes gain-per-unit-cost:

$$r(p) = \frac{g(p)}{c(p)}$$

While intuitive, the ratio criterion has a fundamental flaw: once the highest-ratio path is found, the robot has no incentive to continue, even if budget remains.

3. Expected Gain (ours). We propose a criterion that explicitly accounts for exploration by incorporating frontier information. Let $\mathcal{F}$ denote the set of frontier nodes (nodes leading to graph expansion), and $\mathcal{P}_\mathcal{F}$ the set of paths ending at frontier nodes:

$$g_e(p) = \begin{cases} r(p) \cdot C, & \text{if } p \in \mathcal{P}_\mathcal{F} \\ g(p), & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

For frontier paths, the expected gain extrapolates the current gain-to-cost ratio over the full budget $C$, estimating the potential future gain if the robot continues from the frontier into newly discovered regions. For non-frontier paths, it defaults to the path gain. This naturally balances exploration (via frontier extrapolation) and exploitation (via direct gain).

Equivalently, using the maximum frontier ratio $r_\mathcal{F}^* = \max_{p \in \mathcal{P}_\mathcal{F}} r(p)$:

$$\max_{p \in \mathcal{P}} \; g(p) + \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{P}_\mathcal{F}}(p) \cdot r_\mathcal{F}^* \left(C - c(p)\right)$$

We prove that selecting the path with the highest individual ratio is equivalent to using the global maximum ratio $r_\mathcal{F}^*$ for extrapolation, so the optimal path can be determined without precomputing $r_\mathcal{F}^*$.

Rapidly-Exploring Random Annulus Graph (RRAG)

RRAG is an incrementally built graph for active perception, inspired by the Rapidly-exploring Random Graph (RRG). It enforces two spacing rules that give it an annulus structure:

  • Minimum distance $l_{\min}$: a new node is accepted only if its distance to all existing nodes exceeds $l_{\min}$, preventing oversampling.
  • Maximum distance $l_{\max}$: edges are formed only between nodes within distance $l_{\max}$, bounding the out-degree.

Algorithm: RRAG Construction

Input: Current graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$

  1. for $i = 1$ to $n_{\text{new}}$ do
    1. $v_{\text{new}} \leftarrow$ SampleFree()   [reject if within $l_{\min}$ of any existing node; relocate if beyond $l_{\max}$ from nearest]
    2. if $v_{\text{new}}$ is invalid: continue
    3. $v_{\text{nearest}} \leftarrow$ Nearest$(\mathcal{V}, v_{\text{new}})$
    4. if CollisionFree$(v_{\text{nearest}}, v_{\text{new}})$ then
      1. $\mathcal{V}_{\text{near}} \leftarrow$ Near$(\mathcal{V}, v_{\text{new}}, l_{\max})$
      2. for each $v_{\text{near}} \in \mathcal{V}_{\text{near}}$ do
        • if CollisionFree$(v_{\text{near}}, v_{\text{new}})$: $\mathcal{E} \leftarrow \mathcal{E} \cup \{(v_{\text{near}}, v_{\text{new}})\}$
        • if CollisionFree$(v_{\text{new}}, v_{\text{near}})$: $\mathcal{E} \leftarrow \mathcal{E} \cup \{(v_{\text{new}}, v_{\text{near}})\}$
      3. $\mathcal{V} \leftarrow \mathcal{V} \cup \{v_{\text{new}}\}$
  2. return $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$

Key properties:

  • Full orientation preservation. Unlike prior methods that keep only one orientation per position, RRAG retains all discrete orientations as distinct nodes within a node cluster, enabling richer path optimization.
  • Fallback local planner. In narrow or cluttered environments where straight-line connections fail, a fallback local sampling-based planner (FLS) based on RRT* finds collision-free paths, ensuring graph connectivity.
  • Guaranteed connectivity. We prove that the annulus graph is connected whenever $l_{\max} \geq 2\,l_{\min}$, provided the nodes satisfy packing and covering conditions.

Results

Active Perception Performance

NBS achieves the highest normalized performance across all three representative active perception tasks: point collection, surface reconstruction, and volumetric exploration. It outperforms state-of-the-art methods (RRT, RRT*, SPT, TSP, DBS, FUEL) by at least 20% in one or more tasks. NBS is particularly strong in point collection, where the sparse gain distribution causes other methods to get trapped in local optima.

Surface Reconstruction Comparison

Qualitative comparison of surface reconstruction quality across planners. NBS achieves the most complete coverage (445.73 m²), significantly outperforming alternatives such as RRT* (301.75 m²) and FUEL (341.03 m²).

Hardware Experiments

We validate our approach on the ANYmal quadruped robot equipped with a ZED X Mini camera, running the full planning and perception pipeline onboard. Experiments span autonomous surface reconstruction in indoor/outdoor environments and volumetric exploration in a cafeteria and lab.
